The Secret to Perfect Sausage Rolls Every Time

February 14, 2026 7 min read Croydon Bake House
Back to Blog

A good sausage roll is one of those things that sounds simple until you actually try to nail it. The pastry has to be flaky, not soggy. The filling has to be well-seasoned and moist without being greasy. And they have to hold together when you pick them up. Get any one of those things wrong and you can tell.

We sell a lot of sausage rolls at Croydon Bake House, and they are consistently one of the first things to go in the morning. Over time we have worked out exactly what makes the difference between a sausage roll that is just fine and one that people come back for. This is our recipe, and it works.

Classic Pork and Herb Sausage Rolls

Prep time 25 min
Cook time 25 min
Makes 12 rolls
Difficulty Easy

For the Filling

  • 500g pork mince
  • 200g beef mince
  • 1 medium brown onion, finely grated
  • 2 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 cup fresh breadcrumbs (not dry)
  • 1 egg
  • 2 tbsp Worcestershire sauce
  • 1 tsp dried thyme
  • 1 tsp dried sage
  • 1 tsp smoked paprika
  • 1.5 tsp salt
  • Generous amount of cracked black pepper

For Assembly

  • 3 sheets frozen puff pastry, thawed
  • 1 egg, beaten with 1 tbsp water (egg wash)
  • Sesame seeds for topping (optional)

Why the Filling Mix Matters

Pure pork mince on its own can be a bit dense. The blend of pork and beef lightens it slightly and adds a richer, more savoury depth. The ratio matters: too much beef and you lose the sweetness of the pork; too little and the texture becomes heavy.

The breadcrumbs are not filler. They keep the inside moist as it cooks by absorbing and holding the fat that renders out of the mince. Fresh breadcrumbs work better than dried because they integrate more smoothly into the mix. You can make them by blitzing a couple of slices of white bread in a food processor.

Grating the onion rather than dicing it is a small thing that makes a big difference. Diced onion can create uneven patches in the filling and sometimes stays slightly crunchy. Grated onion dissolves into the mix and seasons every bite evenly.

Mixing the Filling

  1. Combine all filling ingredients in a large bowl. Use your hands to mix thoroughly for about 2 minutes. You want everything fully incorporated and the mixture should feel slightly sticky and hold together when you squeeze it.
  2. Fry off a small spoonful of the mixture in a pan to test the seasoning before rolling. It sounds like an extra step but it is worth it. The filling going into the pastry is exactly what your customer will taste. Adjust salt, pepper or Worcestershire to your liking.
  3. Refrigerate the filling for 20 minutes. Chilled filling is firmer and much easier to roll neatly into the pastry.
The most important tip in this recipe

Always cook a test piece before you commit to rolling all 12. Seasoning raw mince is difficult to gauge by taste alone. A two-minute test fry tells you exactly what you have and saves a whole batch from being underseasoned.

Working with Puff Pastry

Store-bought puff pastry is completely fine for sausage rolls. The layers are already built in. What matters is how you handle it.

  • Thaw the pastry in the fridge, not at room temperature. It should be cold and pliable when you use it, not warm and sticky.
  • Work one sheet at a time. Keep the others in the fridge until you need them.
  • Do not stretch the pastry when you lay it out. Stretched pastry shrinks back in the oven and your rolls will be misshapen.

Rolling and Sealing

  1. Preheat your oven to 210°C (190°C fan-forced) and line two baking trays with baking paper.
  2. Cut each sheet of puff pastry in half lengthways so you have two long rectangles from each sheet.
  3. Divide the filling into six equal portions. Shape each one into a log running the full length of one pastry rectangle, about 3cm in from one edge. The filling log should be compact and even, roughly 3cm in diameter.
  4. Brush the far edge of the pastry with egg wash. Fold the near edge over the filling and roll firmly, finishing with the seam side down. Press along the seam gently to make sure it is sealed.
  5. Cut each roll into two pieces. Place on the lined tray with space between them. Brush the tops with egg wash and scatter with sesame seeds if you like. Score the tops lightly with a sharp knife to allow steam to escape.
  6. Bake for 22 to 25 minutes until the pastry is deep golden and the filling is cooked through. The base should be crisp, not pale. If the tops are browning faster than the base, drop the tray to a lower shelf for the final 5 minutes.
Getting the base crisp

A pale, soft base is the most common complaint with homemade sausage rolls. Use a dark-coloured metal tray rather than a light or thin one. Dark trays absorb more heat and transfer it more effectively to the bottom of the pastry. A pizza stone works even better if you have one.

Common Mistakes and How to Fix Them

The filling falls out

Usually caused by the seam being on top instead of underneath, or not enough egg wash on the seam to act as glue. Roll the pastry firmly around the filling and make sure the seam is pressed down onto the tray.

The pastry is soggy on the bottom

Either the oven is not hot enough, the tray is too thin, or the pastry was too warm when it went in. Make sure your oven is fully preheated and work with cold pastry straight from the fridge.

The filling is dry and crumbly

The fat content in the mince was too low, or the breadcrumbs were left out. Stick to regular pork mince rather than extra-lean, and always include the breadcrumbs.

The rolls split along the side

The filling was overpacked. Leave a clear edge of pastry on both sides of the filling log so there is enough to seal and still allow for a little expansion as the meat cooks.

A Spinach and Ricotta Version

We also make a spinach and ricotta roll at the bakehouse, and it has built up a solid following. The method is the same, but the filling is very different.

For the vegetarian version, combine 250g firm ricotta, 150g frozen spinach (thawed and squeezed completely dry), 2 eggs, 80g grated parmesan, salt, pepper, a grating of fresh nutmeg and a small handful of chopped fresh basil. The filling should be thick enough to hold its shape when scooped. If it is too loose, add another tablespoon of parmesan or a little extra breadcrumb. Roll and bake exactly the same way, but check a little earlier as the vegetarian filling does not need as long to cook through.

Homemade sausage rolls are genuinely one of the easiest things to get right once you understand what the filling needs and how to handle the pastry. Make a batch on the weekend and see how long they last.
