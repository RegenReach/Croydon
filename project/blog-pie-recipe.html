How to Bake a Classic Aussie Meat Pie from Scratch

March 1, 2026 8 min read Croydon Bake House
Back to Blog

There is something deeply satisfying about pulling a golden meat pie out of the oven. The pastry cracks when you press it. The filling steams up. The whole kitchen smells like a proper Australian bakery. It is one of those things you cannot replicate from a supermarket freezer, no matter how convincing the packaging looks.

We bake hundreds of pies every morning at Croydon Bake House, and we have picked up a few things along the way. This recipe is our guide to making a classic beef pie at home. It is not complicated, but there are a handful of steps where the details really matter. We will flag those as we go.

Classic Aussie Meat Pie

Prep time 30 min
Cook time 1 hr 20 min
Makes 6 individual pies
Difficulty Moderate

For the Beef Filling

  • 600g beef mince (not too lean, 80/20 fat ratio works well)
  • 1 brown onion, finely diced
  • 2 cloves garlic, minced
  • 2 tbsp tomato paste
  • 1 tbsp Worcestershire sauce
  • 1 cup beef stock
  • 2 tbsp plain flour
  • Salt and black pepper to taste
  • 1 tbsp olive oil

For the Shortcrust Base

  • 250g plain flour
  • 125g cold butter, cubed
  • 1 egg yolk
  • 2 to 3 tbsp cold water
  • Pinch of salt

For the Lid

  • 2 sheets ready-rolled puff pastry (store-bought is fine)
  • 1 egg, beaten (for egg wash)

Making the Filling

The filling is where a lot of home bakers go wrong. They rush it. Mince that is cooked and seasoned in ten minutes tastes like it was cooked in ten minutes. You need time for the liquid to reduce and the flavours to come together properly.

  1. Heat olive oil in a heavy-based pan over medium heat. Add the onion and cook for 6 to 8 minutes until soft and just starting to colour. Add the garlic and cook for another minute.
  2. Turn the heat up to medium-high. Add the mince in batches, breaking it up with a wooden spoon. Do not crowd the pan or it will steam instead of brown. Brown each batch before adding the next.
  3. Return all the mince to the pan. Add the flour and stir well to coat, cooking for 2 minutes. This helps thicken the filling later without making it gluey.
  4. Add the tomato paste and Worcestershire sauce. Stir to combine, then pour in the beef stock.
  5. Reduce the heat to low. Simmer uncovered for 30 to 40 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the filling is thick and saucy. It should hold its shape when you spoon it, not run across the plate. Season well with salt and pepper.
  6. Spread the filling onto a tray and refrigerate until completely cold. Do not skip this step. Hot filling in a pastry case will make the base soggy and the pastry will not cook properly.
Baker's note

The filling can be made the day before and stored in the fridge overnight. It actually tastes better the next day once the flavours have had time to develop.

Making the Shortcrust Base

You can use store-bought shortcrust for the base if you are short on time, and it will still produce a decent result. But making it from scratch gives you better control over the thickness and texture, and it is not difficult.

  1. Sift the flour and salt into a large bowl. Add the cold butter cubes. Using your fingertips, rub the butter into the flour until the mixture resembles coarse breadcrumbs. Work quickly so the butter stays cold.
  2. Make a well in the centre. Add the egg yolk and 2 tablespoons of cold water. Mix with a flat-bladed knife until the dough just comes together, adding the extra water if needed. Do not overwork it.
  3. Shape into a disc, wrap in plastic wrap and refrigerate for at least 30 minutes.
Why cold butter matters

Cold butter creates small pockets of fat in the dough. When those hit the oven heat, they release steam and create the flaky texture you want. Warm butter blends in too fully and the pastry ends up tough.

Assembling and Baking the Pies

  1. Preheat your oven to 200°C (180°C fan-forced). Grease 6 individual pie tins, roughly 10cm wide.
  2. Roll the chilled shortcrust pastry to about 3mm thick on a lightly floured surface. Cut circles slightly larger than your tins and press them in carefully, making sure the pastry goes up the sides. Trim any excess.
  3. Blind bake the bases for 10 minutes. To do this, line each base with baking paper and fill with rice or baking weights. This stops the base from puffing up before the filling goes in. Remove the weights and paper, then bake for a further 5 minutes until just starting to colour.
  4. Spoon the cold filling into each case, filling to just below the rim. Do not overfill or the lid will lift and crack when it bakes.
  5. Cut puff pastry lids to fit. Press the edges down firmly and crimp with a fork to seal. Brush with egg wash and cut a small steam vent in the centre of each lid.
  6. Bake for 20 to 25 minutes until the pastry is deep golden and the filling is bubbling through the vent. Leave to rest in the tins for 5 minutes before turning out.

Tips for Getting It Right

  • Use cold filling. Warm filling is the number one reason home-baked pies end up with a soggy base.
  • Do not skip blind baking. That short pre-bake gives the base a head start so it does not go soft under the weight of the filling.
  • Rest the pastry. Chilling the shortcrust before rolling relaxes the gluten and stops it from shrinking in the tin.
  • Go generous with seasoning. Pie filling needs more salt than you think. Taste it before it goes cold and adjust.
  • Use full-fat beef mince. Lean mince dries out and goes grainy. A bit of fat keeps the filling moist and flavourful.

Variations Worth Trying

Once you have the base recipe sorted, there is a lot of room to experiment. At the bakehouse we make steak and cheese, pepper beef, butter chicken, shepherd's pie and more. The method is largely the same. The flavour profile is where you can have fun.

  • Steak and cheese: Use diced chuck steak instead of mince, braise it low and slow for an hour, then top the filling with a layer of grated cheddar before putting the lid on.
  • Pepper beef: Add 1 to 2 teaspoons of cracked black pepper and a tablespoon of dijon mustard to the basic filling. It sounds simple but the result is genuinely different.
  • Butter chicken: Replace the beef with diced chicken thigh and use a butter chicken sauce instead of the stock base. This one always sells out first at the bakehouse.

Baking a good pie takes a bit of time and a bit of patience, but it is one of the most satisfying things you can make at home. If you want to try the real thing before you get baking, you know where to find us.
